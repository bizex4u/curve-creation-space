ATL (Above the Line) and BTL (Below the Line) is the oldest distinction in Indian advertising planning — and one of the most misunderstood. Brands regularly allocate budgets to one or the other without a clear framework for why or how the two should work together.

This guide explains the difference, when each applies in the Indian market and how to build a campaign strategy that integrates both.

## What ATL and BTL actually mean

The terms originate from old accounting practice — advertising agencies used to draw a literal line in their budget sheets separating media that earned commission (above) from promotional activities that didn't (below).

Today the distinction is simpler:

**ATL (Above the Line)**: Mass media — television, radio, cinema, print, outdoor (OOH). The goal is reach — communicating to a broad, undifferentiated audience. ATL builds brand awareness and top-of-mind recall at scale.

**BTL (Below the Line)**: Direct, targeted activities — in-store promotions, sampling, direct mail, trade activations, point-of-sale materials, events and experiential marketing. The goal is conversion — driving specific behaviour from a defined audience segment.

A third category — **TTL (Through the Line)** — is increasingly common. Digital advertising and DOOH blur the ATL/BTL distinction: digital can be mass (YouTube, Meta reach campaigns) or highly targeted (performance campaigns with narrow audience parameters).

## ATL advertising formats in India

### Television
India has 900+ TV channels across national and regional languages. Prime time GRPs command the highest CPMs. Television remains the highest-reach ATL format for mass-market consumer goods — particularly in rural and semi-urban India where digital penetration remains lower.

**Costs**: 30-second prime time spot on major Hindi GEC: ₹1.5–5 lakh per spot. Regional channels: ₹10,000–1 lakh per spot.

### Radio
FM radio reaches urban and semi-urban audiences efficiently. Drive-time (7–9 AM, 5–7 PM) is peak for commuters — strong for local brand messaging, promotional announcements and hyperlocal retail campaigns.

**Costs**: 30-second prime time spot, major metro FM: ₹15,000–60,000 per spot.

### Cinema
Pre-show TVC screening in multiplexes — captive audience, large-format screen, emotional engagement context. Strong for premium brands and new product launches. See our [cinema advertising guide](/blog/cinema-advertising-india) for full detail.

### Print (newspaper and magazine)
National dailies (Times of India, Hindustan Times, Hindu) and Hindi-language dailies (Dainik Bhaskar, Dainik Jagran) for mass national reach. Declining but still relevant for Tier 2 and 3 market reach.

**Costs**: Full-page TOI front page: ₹40–80 lakh. Half-page metro edition: ₹5–20 lakh.

### Outdoor (OOH)
Hoardings, unipoles, gantries and street furniture across India's cities and highways. OOH is one of the most cost-efficient ATL formats for city-level brand presence. DOOH (digital OOH) adds frequency management and dynamic content capabilities. See our [outdoor advertising guide](/blog/outdoor-advertising-india-buyers-playbook).

**Costs**: Major metro hoarding: ₹2–20 lakh per month. See city-specific rates in our OOH guide.

## BTL advertising formats in India

### In-store and trade activations
Point-of-sale displays, gondola ends, shelf strips, floor stickers and branded display units at retail. India's traditional trade (kirana stores, general trade) still accounts for 75%+ of FMCG sales — BTL at trade level directly influences purchase decisions.

### Sampling and experiential
Product sampling in malls, markets, residential complexes and transit hubs. The highest-conversion BTL format — trial drives first purchase better than any awareness format.

**Costs**: Mall sampling activation: ₹50,000–3 lakh per event (location, staffing, product cost).

### Direct mail and flyer distribution
Area-specific flyer drops, mailer campaigns and door-to-door. Still relevant for hyperlocal retail, residential property and service businesses.

### Events and sponsorships
Brand association with cultural events, sports, corporate events. Experiential touchpoints that create emotional connection beyond awareness.

### Digital BTL
Performance marketing (Meta, Google) with narrow audience targeting. Email campaigns, WhatsApp marketing, influencer activations with specific audience segments. BTL principles applied to digital channels.

## ATL vs BTL: the key differences

| Dimension | ATL | BTL |
|-----------|-----|-----|
| Primary goal | Awareness, reach | Conversion, trial |
| Audience targeting | Broad, mass | Specific, defined |
| Measurability | Harder (recall, awareness) | Easier (trial, redemption, sales lift) |
| Timeline | Brand building (months/years) | Campaign activation (days/weeks) |
| Cost structure | High absolute, low CPM | Variable, often high per-contact |
| Best for | New markets, brand building | Launch conversion, loyalty, trade |

## When to use ATL in India

**Market entry or geographic expansion**: ATL creates awareness before distribution is in place — essential for building pull demand that incentivises trade stocking.

**Brand repositioning**: Changing consumer perception (moving upmarket, extending into new categories) requires mass communication at scale. BTL alone can't move brand image.

**Seasonal peaks**: Diwali, wedding season, IPL — high-consumption periods where share of voice drives share of market. ATL investment during peak seasons delivers disproportionate recall.

**FMCG launches**: For impulse or low-involvement categories, awareness-to-trial gap is narrow — high ATL reach primes consumers before they encounter the product at retail.

## When to use BTL in India

**Driving trial in distribution markets**: Product already in stores — now need to convert awareness to first purchase. Sampling at retail, in-store activation, trade incentives.

**Hyperlocal targeting**: New stores, specific residential catchments, event-based marketing. BTL delivers where ATL is over-targeted.

**Trade relationship building**: India's distribution ecosystem requires active trade engagement — dealer meets, retailer incentives and in-store visibility programmes are BTL activities that drive distribution depth.

**Retention and loyalty**: Existing customers respond better to targeted BTL (loyalty rewards, personalised offers, direct communication) than mass ATL.

## The integrated approach: ATL + BTL

The most effective Indian campaigns combine ATL and BTL in a sequential or simultaneous strategy:

### Sequential model
1. **ATL first**: Build awareness and brand recognition with OOH, cinema or radio
2. **BTL activation**: Convert awareness to trial with in-store, sampling and trade push
3. **ATL maintenance**: Keep top-of-mind during conversion window

This model works well for new product launches or geographic expansion. Awareness must precede conversion.

### Simultaneous model
ATL and BTL run concurrently — ATL targets consumers at home and in transit; BTL targets them at point of purchase. Reinforcement effect: consumer who saw your hoarding on the way to the mall encounters your sampling activation inside.

This model works well for established brands with existing awareness — the BTL amplifies an ATL presence already in market.

### Budget allocation in India

As a rough starting framework for brand-building campaigns:

| Campaign stage | ATL % | BTL % |
|----------------|-------|-------|
| New launch | 60–70% | 30–40% |
| Established brand, new market | 50–60% | 40–50% |
| Mature market, loyalty focus | 30–40% | 60–70% |
| Seasonal activation | 70–80% | 20–30% |

## The DOOH middle ground

Digital OOH sits between ATL and BTL in its capabilities:

- **Broad reach** (ATL behaviour): City-wide or network DOOH delivers mass impressions
- **Audience targeting** (BTL-like): Geo-targeted DOOH can select specific locations based on audience profile
- **Frequency management** (BTL-like): Slot-based booking lets you control daily impression frequency
- **Dynamic content** (BTL-like): Day-parting and content triggers allow message customisation

## Common mistakes in ATL/BTL planning

**Treating ATL and BTL as either/or**: In practice, consumer purchase journeys require both awareness and conversion touchpoints. Brands that only do ATL build awareness without conversion; brands that only do BTL convert existing audiences without growing new ones.

**Not aligning timing**: ATL awareness campaigns that run 4–6 weeks before BTL activations waste reach. By the time sampling or in-store activation begins, awareness generated by ATL has decayed.

**Underinvesting in BTL trade support**: India's traditional trade distribution requires active BTL at the retailer and distributor level. FMCG brands that invest heavily in consumer ATL but neglect trade BTL often see awareness without sales velocity.

**Measuring ATL by BTL metrics**: ATL campaigns should be measured on reach, frequency and brand recall — not on direct sales attribution. Expecting direct ROI measurement from a cinema campaign or OOH hoarding sets the wrong success criteria.
